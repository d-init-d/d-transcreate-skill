# Style Sheet

## Voice

### Register
Literary — elevated prose with accessible vocabulary; avoid academic density.

### Formality
Moderate-high formality in narration; relaxed in dialogue. Use full verb forms in exposition; allow contractions and colloquial rhythm in character speech.

### Sentence Rhythm
Mirror source paragraph length. Prefer complex subordination in descriptive passages to maintain atmospheric tension. Use short, punchy sentences for moments of action or revelation.

### Genre Constraints
Maintain dark fantasy tone throughout. Avoid modern slang or anachronistic expressions. Preserve the sense of ancient mystery and quiet dread. The world should feel old and layered.

### Notes
The source text uses a restrained, literary voice — atmospheric rather than action-driven. Vietnamese translation should lean toward the lyrical tradition of Vietnamese fantasy prose (truyện kỳ ảo) while remaining contemporary and readable. Avoid the overly ornate style of classical Vietnamese literature.

## Language Conventions

### Dialogue Punctuation
Use em-dash (—) to open dialogue lines, following Vietnamese literary convention. Use standard quotation marks ("...") only for inner quotes or quoted terms. Comma before dialogue tag when tag follows speech.

### Titles and Headings
Translate scene headings. Use sentence case. Preserve "Scene X" numbering as "Cảnh X".

### Names
Keep character names in source form: Seren, Moth, Kael Morrow. Keep place names that function as proper nouns: Ashveil, Thornwood. Translate descriptive place names: Sorrow River → sông Sầu, Salt Cliffs → Vách Muối.

### Terms of Address
Moth addresses Seren as a peer/ward — use "cậu" (Moth → Seren) to convey the familiar-but-slightly-parental dynamic. Seren's internal narration uses neutral self-reference.

### Honorifics
N/A — no honorific system in this text. Characters use first names only.

### Number, Date, and Unit Conventions
N/A — no specific numbers, dates, or units appear in the source text.

### Notes
The Moth-Seren dynamic is key: Moth is ancient but speaks casually, like a weary mentor. The Vietnamese pronoun choice (Moth using "cậu" for Seren) establishes this without explicit exposition. Seren's dialogue should feel determined and young — direct sentences, few hedging words.

## Adaptation Rules

### Idioms
Replace English idioms with Vietnamese equivalents that preserve tone. Example: "left it as a question mark forever" — adapt to convey the same sense of deliberate ignorance without forcing a literal translation of the metaphor.

### Humor
Minimal humor in source. Moth's dry observations carry light irony — preserve the understated quality rather than amplifying for comedic effect.

### Cultural References
No culture-specific references requiring adaptation. The fantasy world is self-contained.

### Metaphors
Preserve source metaphors when comprehensible in Vietnamese. Key metaphors to maintain: "folded space like a tailor folds cloth" — the textile/craft metaphor works cross-culturally. "Holes cut in the shape of people" — preserve the visual precision of this image.

### Repetition
Preserve deliberate structural repetition (e.g., "Three paths that..." in scene 3). This is rhetorical and intentional.

### Notes
The story relies heavily on spatial and visual metaphors (maps, paths, light, absence). These translate well into Vietnamese without adaptation. The key challenge is maintaining the atmospheric tension through sentence rhythm rather than vocabulary — Vietnamese prose can achieve this through careful clause ordering and particle placement.

## Forbidden Patterns

| Pattern | Reason |
|---------|--------|
| Modern Vietnamese slang (e.g., "ảo thật đấy", "chill") | Breaks fantasy atmosphere and literary register |
| Sino-Vietnamese compounds where native Vietnamese exists and sounds natural | Over-formalization damages readability for general audience |
| Passive voice in narration where active voice is natural in Vietnamese | Translatorese — Vietnamese strongly prefers active constructions |
| Literal calque of "the way a [noun] [verbs] [noun]" simile structure | Restructure to Vietnamese-natural comparison patterns |
| Mixing "tôi/mình/ta" self-reference within a single character's speech | Breaks voice consistency — each character gets one self-reference form |

### Notes
Vietnamese fantasy prose has its own conventions distinct from translated-fiction style. The goal is a text that reads as if originally written in Vietnamese, not as a translation. Avoid the "dịch thuật" (translation-ese) quality that marks many translated fantasy novels in the Vietnamese market.
